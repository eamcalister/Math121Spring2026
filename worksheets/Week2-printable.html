    Example 1.

    Hereâ€™s another way to find the vertex of a quadratic using simpler functions and shifts. Consider the function
    \begin{equation*} h(x) = 2x^2-6x+20. \end{equation*}
    We can write this as a vertical shift of the function
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = 2x^2-6x \end{equation*}
    upward by \(20\) units. Notice that
    \begin{align*} f(x) \amp = 2x^2 - 6x\\ \amp = 2x(x-3) \end{align*}
    This means \(f(x)\) (from which \(h(x)\) came) has easily identified zeros at \(x=0\) and \(x=3\text{.}\) Its vertex lies halfway between them at \(x=1.5\text{.}\) Then we can compute the \(y\)-value of the vertex of \(f(x)\) to be
    \begin{equation*} f(1.5) = 2\cdot 1.5 \cdot (-1.5) = -4.5. \end{equation*}
    Thus, the vertex of \(h(x)\) is \(20\) units higher at \(y = 15.5\text{.}\) So the vertex form of \(h(x)\) is
    \begin{equation*} h(x) = 2x^2-6x+20 = 2(x-1.5)^2 + 15.5 \end{equation*}
